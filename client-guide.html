A quick guide to getting the most out of our coaching together — how I communicate, how to use our Trello board, and what to expect from our sessions.

01 — Availability

When to Reach Me

I'm available Monday through Saturday, 6 am–8 pm PT (Beijing time: Monday–Saturday, 9 pm–11 am the following morning).

Sundays are off-limits — I protect that day for myself, and I hope you'll protect your rest days too. Outside of that window, I'm genuinely reachable. The best way to surface something for my attention is to tag me on Trello — more on that in a moment.

02 — Communication

How I Communicate

I will be direct with you. When your essay isn't working, I'll tell you plainly — not to be harsh, but because vague feedback wastes your time. Honesty is how I show respect.

And I am genuinely rooting for you. My directness comes from care, not criticism. Please always assume positive intent — if something I say lands oddly, just tell me. I want to get it right.

I may throw several ideas at you at once — frameworks, story angles, essay approaches, school list thoughts all at the same time. This is me trusting your ability to hold a lot of ideas.

If I'm moving too fast, just say: "Can we slow down?" I'll follow your lead.

03 — Trello

How to Use Trello

Your Trello board is the home base for everything — drafts, exercises, timelines, school research. But there's one habit that makes the whole system work:

Tag me with @ on every card where you want my eyes.
I check Trello regularly, but I'm not scrolling every card — I'm watching for mentions. If you upload something without tagging me, I may genuinely miss it. That's not me ignoring you; it's just how Trello works. The @ sign is how we make sure nothing falls through the cracks.

In practice, that looks like this:

  • Upload a new essay draft → leave a comment tagging @lisaqiyali with the version number and what you'd like feedback on
  • Have a question between sessions → open a card, write your question, @ mention me
  • Finish an exercise like the Superpower Exercise → leave a comment tagging me so I know it's ready to review

I will always respond.

04 — Files

Version Control

I am very particular about this. We will go through many rounds of revision on each document — sometimes 5, 6, or 8 versions. Without a clear naming system, files get lost, the wrong version gets submitted, or we spend half a session figuring out which draft we're looking at. Follow this convention from Day 1:

Naming format: [YourName]_[DocumentType]_v[number].docx
Filename Who What it means
WangFang_Resume_v1.docx You Your first draft
WangFang_Resume_v1 (LL).docx Lisa Lisa's edited version of your v1
WangFang_Resume_v2.docx You Your revision incorporating Lisa's feedback
WangFang_Resume_v2 (LL).docx Lisa Lisa's edits on v2
WangFang_Goals_Essay_HBS_v1.docx You Your first draft of the HBS goals essay
WangFang_Goals_Essay_HBS_v1 (LL).docx Lisa Lisa's edits on that draft

The logic is simple: you increment the number (v1, v2, v3…). I append (LL) to whatever version you sent me. We can both see at a glance who last touched the file and where we are in the process.

  • Never save over a previous version — always create a new numbered file
  • Upload every new version as a Trello attachment and @ mention me
05 — Philosophy

My Coaching Philosophy

This process is about uncovering who you already are — not constructing a version of you that sounds impressive. The best MBA applications don't read like resumes in essay form. They read like a person you'd want to be stuck in an airport with for three hours.

I Your superpower is real.

It's the thing people always come to you for — that feels almost too easy to you. We'll find it. Everything flows from there.

II Action before motivation.

Don't wait until you feel ready to write. Write badly first. The feeling of momentum comes from starting, not the other way around.

III Stories beat statements.

"I am a leader" is a claim. "Here's what happened in that room" is a story. Stories win.

IV Leadership doesn't require a title.

Some of your best stories might be the quiet ones — the meeting where you named the tension no one else would, the colleague you invested in. Don't dismiss them.

06 — Sessions

How Our Sessions Work

Come prepared. Share any new drafts, exercises, or thinking at least 24 hours before our call by uploading to Trello and tagging me. This lets me actually read your work in advance — which means we spend the session going deep, not me reading out loud while you watch.

Leave with one clear next step. Every session should end with a specific action you're taking before we talk next. If you leave a session unsure what to do, tell me — I'll fix that before we hang up.

We aim for at least one Zoom call per week — typically an hour to an hour and a half, and I'll add time when we need it. My goal is to make sure you walk away with real clarity, not to watch the clock. And Zoom isn't the only way to reach me — drop a question on WeChat anytime; I will always respond (as long as I'm awake). Voice notes are great too. For anything that can wait, leave it in Trello and we'll work through it asynchronously.

07 — Getting Unstuck

When You're Stuck

Staring at a blank page is one of the hardest parts of this process. It will happen. Here's what actually helps:

  1. Step away from the page. Take a walk. Clear your mind. You'd be surprised how much your brain keeps working when you stop forcing it.
  2. Talk instead of write. Use dictation software — I love Wispr Flow, and there are other great options. When you talk out loud, your voice emerges naturally. You start to understand who you are by describing yourself out loud. Things come out that would never come out on a keyboard.
  3. Remember: writing is the last step. This process is really one of self-exploration. Thinking comes first. Talking comes second. Writing is just capturing what's already there. If you're stuck, you probably haven't done enough thinking yet — and that's okay.
Don't optimize the draft that doesn't exist yet.
